## Round 1: Product Manager Interview (45 min)

> **Interviewer**: Jennifer Liu (PM Lead, Google Maps)  
> **Focus**: Product Sense + Structured Thinking  
> **Date**: 2026-01-18

### Raw Transcript (Messy Notes)

```
started 5 min late, she apologized — seemed nice, relaxed vibe

Q1: Tell me about yourself and why PM?
talked about my CS background but always more interested in the "why" than the "how"
mentioned my side project — a campus food ordering app
she nodded when I said "shipped to 2000 users"
(should've mentioned the retention metrics here, forgot)

Q2: Pick a Google product and tell me what you'd improve
I chose Google Maps — said the "Explore" tab feels cluttered
my suggestion: personalized recommendations based on past behavior
she pushed back: "what if users don't want Google knowing their habits?"
I fumbled a bit... said something about privacy controls and opt-in
(not my best answer, felt defensive)

Q3: Design a feature for college students using Google Calendar
this one I liked!
- started with user persona: busy student, multiple classes, social events
- pain point: hard to balance academic + social
- my idea: "Focus Blocks" — auto-suggest study time based on assignment deadlines
- she asked about success metrics
- I said: adoption rate, user-reported stress levels, GPA correlation (joked a bit)
she laughed — good sign?
- follow-up: how would you prioritize this vs other features?
- I used RICE framework... reach, impact, confidence, effort
- said this would have high reach for student segment but maybe low for overall userbase
(felt pretty good about this one)

Q4: estimation question - caught me off guard
How many photos are uploaded to Google Photos per day?
ugh I hate these
- global smartphone users... 4 billion?
- maybe 30% use Google Photos? = 1.2B users
- photos per user per day... some upload 0, power users upload 10+
- average maybe 2? so... 2.4 billion photos/day?
she said "interesting approach" — no idea if I was close
(need to practice more estimation)

Q5: Tell me about a time you failed
talked about my hackathon project that crashed during demo
- we didn't test edge cases
- learned: always have a backup plan, test under real conditions
she asked: what would you do differently now?
I said: build in buffer time, do a full dry run
(standard answer but genuine)

Q6: Why Google? Why APM specifically?
- APM program's rotation model appeals to me
- want to learn from senior PMs
- Google's scale means even small improvements = huge impact
- mentioned I read "Inspired" and loved the chapter on Google's PM culture
(she seemed to appreciate the book reference)

ending:
she said team will discuss, recruiter will reach out in ~2 weeks
asked if I had questions — I asked about her favorite project
she talked about a Maps accessibility feature, seemed passionate

overall feeling: 70% positive?
product design question went well
estimation was rough
need to be more confident when pushed back on
```

## Round 2: Analytical / Cross-functional Interview (45 min)

> **Interviewer**: David Kim (Senior PM, Google Workspace)  
> **Focus**: Data Analysis + Stakeholder Management  
> **Date**: 2026-01-25

### Raw Transcript (Messy Notes)

```
video call, his camera was off for first 2 min (awkward)
when it came on he looked tired — hoped that wouldn't affect my eval

Q1: Walk me through how you'd analyze the success of a new feature launch
I tried to be structured:
1. define success criteria upfront — what does "good" look like?
2. set up tracking: DAU, feature adoption, retention impact
3. segment analysis: new vs existing users, mobile vs desktop
4. qualitative: user feedback, NPS, support tickets
he interrupted: "what if the metrics look good but users are complaining?"
I said: metrics might be lagging, or we're measuring wrong thing
need to dig into qualitative data, do user interviews
(felt okay but he seemed skeptical)

Q2: Case study — sudden drop
Gmail's compose button click rate dropped 15% last week. Walk me through how you'd investigate.
tried to stay calm...
- first: is it a real drop or data issue? check logging, compare data sources
- second: isolate variables — is it all platforms or specific? all regions?
- he said: "it's only on mobile Android"
- okay... recent app update? A/B test running? device-specific bug?
- he said: "there was an update 2 weeks ago but the drop just started"
- hmm... delayed effect? maybe UI change users are now noticing?
- I suggested: look at session recordings, heatmaps, user complaints
- he asked: "what's your hypothesis?"
- I guessed: button placement might be less visible after update, users adapting slowly
- he said "interesting" — couldn't tell if good or bad
(this was hard, felt like I was grasping)

Q3: prioritization exercise
You have 3 features to choose from for Google Docs:
A) Real-time translation (high eng effort, high international impact)
B) Better offline mode (medium effort, helps mobile users)  
C) AI writing suggestions (low effort, trendy but unclear value)

which would you prioritize and why?

my answer:
- ruled out C first — low effort but unclear ROI, might be a distraction
- between A and B... asked clarifying question: what's our strategic priority?
- he said: "assume growth in emerging markets"
- then I picked A — translation directly supports that goal
- but acknowledged risk: high eng effort, need strong partnership with eng
- suggested: maybe MVP with top 5 languages first
he nodded — felt like a reasonable answer

Q4: conflict scenario
Imagine eng team says your feature will take 6 months but you promised stakeholders 3 months. What do you do?

I said:
1. first, understand why the estimate is 6 months — scope creep? technical debt?
2. explore options: can we cut scope? phased launch? add resources?
3. if no way to speed up, be honest with stakeholders — explain tradeoffs
4. avoid blame game, focus on solutions
he pushed: "what if your manager pressures you to keep the 3 month promise?"
this was tricky...
I said: I'd advocate for realistic expectations, but ultimately if overruled, document the risks
(not sure if that was the right answer — felt political)

Q5: any questions for me?
asked about biggest challenge for new PMs at Google
he said: "learning to say no — there are too many good ideas"
also asked about work-life balance
he laughed and said "it's manageable but intense during launches"

wrap-up:
he said feedback would come through recruiter
"you did fine" — is that good? bad? no idea

overall feeling: 55% positive?
the Gmail case study was really hard
prioritization was solid
conflict question — not sure if I handled well
he seemed disengaged... maybe just tired?

self-reflection:
- need to practice more case studies, especially metric debugging
- should ask more clarifying questions before diving in
- confidence still shaky when pushed back on
- remember to breathe, slow down
```
